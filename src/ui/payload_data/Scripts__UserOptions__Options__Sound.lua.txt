[description]
Audio quality settings - controls simultaneous sound limits, CPU load management, and audio processing features across Frostbite titles.

[version]
2011 (Battlefield 3 Alpha/Beta)
[notes]
Initial audio quality system with aggressive sound limits.

High Quality (>0.8):
- Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 30
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 50

Low Quality (≤0.8):
- Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 15
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 40

Technical Explanation:

Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount:
- Maximum simultaneous sounds that can be heard by player
- When limit reached, audio system prioritizes based on:
  * Distance from player
  * Sound importance/priority
  * Volume/intensity
- Lower values = more aggressive culling of distant/quiet sounds

Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit:
- Maximum CPU percentage allocated to audio processing
- Controls audio thread's CPU budget
- Higher values = more complex audio mixing, effects, and spatialization
- Lower values = simplified audio processing to free CPU for rendering/gameplay

The high MaxAudibleSoundCount (30 high, 15 low) reflects the chaotic audio environment requiring careful prioritization.
[content]
local quality = settings['AudioQuality']
log:info("setting sound quality : "..quality)
if(quality > 0.8) then
	applySettings([=[
			Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 30
			Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 50
  ]=])
else
	applySettings([=[
			Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 15
			Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 40
  ]=])
end
[/version]

[version]
2011-2015 (Battlefield 3/4/Hardline, NFS, C&C, GW, SWBF Alpha/Beta)
[notes]
Reduced sound limits after Alpha - more conservative approach.

Changes from Alpha/Beta:
- High quality MaxAudibleSoundCount: 30 → 15 (50% reduction)
- Low quality MaxAudibleSoundCount: 15 → 8 (47% reduction)
- CPU load limits adjusted: High 50 (unchanged), Low 40 → 30

High Quality (>0.8):
- Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 15
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 50

Low Quality (≤0.8):
- Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 8
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 30

Why the Reduction:
1. Performance Optimization: Alpha testing revealed 30 sounds was too expensive
2. Audio Prioritization Improved: Better algorithms meant fewer sounds needed
3. Console Parity: Xbox 360/PS3 couldn't handle 30 simultaneous sounds reliably
4. CPU Budget: Needed more headroom for rendering improvements in final release

Technical Impact:
Halving MaxAudibleSoundCount from 30 to 15 means:
- More aggressive sound culling (distant explosions, background ambience dropped first)
- Better performance on lower-end hardware
- Slightly less immersive audio in chaotic 64-player moments
- But: Most players wouldn't notice due to improved prioritization

Games Using This:
- Battlefield 3/4/Hardline
- Need For Speed The Run/Rivals
- Command & Conquer: Generals 2
- PVZ Garden Warfare 1
- Star Wars Battlefront Alpha/Beta

This became the 'standard' Frostbite audio configuration for mid-2010s games.

	applySettings([=[
			Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 15
			Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 50
  ]=])

			Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 8
			Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 30
  ]=])

- Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount 8
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 30

Developer Comment:
'eala-begin etrama' / 'eala-end' indicates EA Los Angeles (Danger Close Studios) divergence

Technical Explanation:

Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadRecovery:
- When audio processing exceeds CPU limit, this controls recovery behavior
- Value of 5 = gradual throttling/recovery over 5 frames
- Prevents audio stuttering from sudden CPU limit violations
- Smooths audio processing load spikes

Why Medal of Honor Needed This:

AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 70 (vs standard 50):
Medal of Honor: Warfighter featured:
- Extremely detailed weapon audio (realistic gun sounds crucial to military shooter)
- Complex environmental audio (urban warfare, indoor/outdoor transitions)
- Heavy use of audio occlusion/obstruction
- Tier 1 operator authenticity demanded high-fidelity audio

The LoadRecovery system suggests:
- MOH had audio processing spikes during intense moments
- Standard hard limit caused audio dropouts
- Recovery system maintained audio quality during load spikes
- Only applied to high quality (competitive advantage acceptable)

EA Los Angeles Context:
Danger Close Studios developed MOH:W after DICE's BF3 engine was established. The 'eala' tags show they customized Frostbite audio beyond DICE's original parameters for their more audio-focused military simulation approach.

[version]
2013 (Army Of Two)
[notes]
Complete audio API overhaul with obstruction testing.

Critical Changes:
- Audio.MaxAudibleSoundCount → Sound.MaxAudibleSoundCount (namespace change)
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit → audio-rwac-cpu-load-limit (kebab-case naming)
- NEW: audio-max-obstruction-tests-per-frame

High Quality (>0.8):
- Sound.MaxAudibleSoundCount 30
- audio-rwac-cpu-load-limit 50
- audio-max-obstruction-tests-per-frame 2

Low Quality (≤0.8):
- Sound.MaxAudibleSoundCount 15
- audio-rwac-cpu-load-limit 60 (counterintuitively HIGHER than high quality)
- audio-max-obstruction-tests-per-frame 1

Technical Explanation:

Namespace Change (Audio → Sound):
Suggests Army of Two used different Frostbite audio backend or earlier fork

Kebab-Case Naming (audio-rwac-cpu-load-limit):
- Earlier/alternative Frostbite naming convention
- 'rwac' possibly = Real-World Audio Culling or similar acronym
- Indicates different audio system implementation

Audio Obstruction Testing:
- Raycasts from audio source to player to detect occlusion
- Walls, obstacles reduce/muffle sound realistically
- audio-max-obstruction-tests-per-frame limits raycasts per frame
- High quality: 2 tests = more accurate occlusion
- Low quality: 1 test = less CPU cost, simpler occlusion

Counterintuitive CPU Limits:
- High quality: 50 limit
- Low quality: 60 limit (HIGHER)
Why? Likely bug or compensates for:
  * Fewer obstruction tests need more mixing headroom
  * Lower MaxAudibleSoundCount (15 vs 30) allows higher CPU per sound
  * Trade-off between sound count and per-sound processing

This version represents a significantly divergent audio system implementation, possibly due to Army of Two using an earlier or heavily customized Frostbite branch.
[content]
local quality = settings['AudioQuality']
log:info("setting sound quality : "..quality)
if(quality > 0.8) then
	applySettings([=[
			Sound.MaxAudibleSoundCount 30
			audio-rwac-cpu-load-limit 50
			audio-max-obstruction-tests-per-frame 2
  ]=])
else
	applySettings([=[
			Sound.MaxAudibleSoundCount 15
			audio-rwac-cpu-load-limit 60
			audio-max-obstruction-tests-per-frame 1
  ]=])
end

[notes]
Simplified audio system - MaxAudibleSoundCount removed.

Critical Change:
Only CPU load limit remains - MaxAudibleSoundCount completely removed.

High Quality (>0.8):
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 50

Low Quality (≤0.8):
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 30

What's Missing:
- No MaxAudibleSoundCount setting
- Audio system handles sound limits automatically

Why This Simplification:

1. Engine Maturity:
   - Frostbite's audio prioritization became sophisticated enough to auto-manage
   - Dynamic sound limiting based on scene complexity
   - No need for hardcoded limits

2. Game-Specific Needs:
   - Dragon Age Inquisition: RPG with less chaotic audio than shooters
   - Need For Speed: Racing audio is more predictable (engine, tires, wind, music)
   - Mirror's Edge Catalyst: Parkour action, simpler soundscape than battlefield

3. Hardware Advancement:
   - PS4/Xbox One generation had more audio processing power
   - Could handle more sounds within CPU budget automatically

Technical Implications:
Without MaxAudibleSoundCount hardcoded:
- Audio engine dynamically allocates sound slots
- Prioritization based on runtime scene analysis
- CPU budget (30/50) becomes the only constraint
- More flexible, adapts to different game moments

This represents Frostbite audio becoming 'smart' enough to not need manual sound count limits.

[version]
2017 (FIFA, NHL, Madden 25)
[notes]
Sports game audio optimization - PC-specific sample rate reduction.

Revolutionary Change:
Sample rate reduction for low-spec PCs - first time audio QUALITY reduced, not just quantity.

Configuration:
Platform-specific: Only applies on Win32 (PC) platform
Quality-specific: Only applies when quality ≤ 0.8 (low settings)

Low Quality PC (Win32 platform, quality ≤0.8):
- Audio.AudioCoreSampleRate 32000 (32kHz)

All Other Configurations:
- No settings applied (engine defaults)

Developer Comment:
'FIFA_BEGIN | BACKPORT:3101658 | avanderstar | 2016-11-10 | (eta 2017.1) audio settings for min spec PCs'

Technical Explanation:

Audio.AudioCoreSampleRate 32000:
- Reduces sample rate from default to 32kHz
- 32kHz = 33% reduction in audio data throughput
- Nyquist theorem: 32kHz captures frequencies up to 16kHz
- Human hearing: 20Hz-20kHz (32kHz is borderline acceptable)

Why This Matters:

Sample Rate Impact:
- Higher sample rate = better audio quality, more CPU/memory
- 32kHz loses some high-frequency detail (cymbals, crowd noise, whistle overtones)
- But: Still acceptable for sports commentary, crowd ambience, basic sound effects

Minimum Spec PC Context:
- FIFA/NHL/Madden target VERY low-end PCs (integrated graphics, old CPUs)
- Sports games have huge market on budget laptops/PCs
- Sample rate reduction allows game to run on Intel HD Graphics, old dual-core CPUs
- Trade-off: Slightly muffled audio for playability on weak hardware

Backport Comment Significance:
- Originally developed for FIFA
- Backported from FIFA 2017.1 to FIFA/NHL/Madden 25
- Shows EA's sports division sharing optimization techniques
- 'eta 2017.1' suggests FIFA 18 development era (2016-2017)

Why Console Version Doesn't Use This:
- Consoles have fixed hardware (sample rate optimized per-platform)
- Only PC has wild hardware variance requiring runtime sample rate adjustment
[content]
local quality = settings['AudioQuality']
log:debug("setting sound quality : "..quality)
-- FIFA_BEGIN | BACKPORT:3101658 | avanderstar | 2016-11-10 | (eta 2017.1) audio settings for min spec PCs
if platform == 'Win32' and quality <= 0.8 then
applySettings([=[
		Audio.AudioCoreSampleRate 32000
  ]=])
-- FIFA_END
end

[notes]
Dragon Age-specific audio with reverb quality system.

Changes from Standard:
- Custom CPU limits with explicit DIVERGENCE tag
- NEW: MorrisonAudio.ReverbQuality setting
- Game-specific audio needs

High Quality (>0.8):
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 80 (increased from 50)
- MorrisonAudio.ReverbQuality ReverbQuality_High

Low Quality (≤0.8):
- Audio.AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 30 (unchanged)
- MorrisonAudio.ReverbQuality ReverbQuality_Optimized

Developer Comment:
'MORRISON_START - 2020/12/07 - Cody Behiel - [DIVERGENCE] Increasing audio core load limit for PC'

Technical Explanation:

AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 80:
- Increased from standard 50 to 80 (60% increase)
- Dragon Age needs higher CPU for complex audio
- Dated 2020/12/07 - mid-development decision

MorrisonAudio.ReverbQuality:
- 'Morrison' = Dragon Age: The Veilguard's codename
- Game-specific audio namespace
- Controls reverb (echo/room acoustics) quality

ReverbQuality_High:
- Full reverb simulation
- Accurate room acoustics
- Multiple reflection calculations
- Tail decay modeling

ReverbQuality_Optimized:
- Simplified reverb algorithms
- Fewer reflections calculated
- Faster decay, less CPU cost
- Still acceptable acoustic immersion

AudioCoreCpuLoadLimit 80:
Allows Dragon Age to:
- Play multiple companion dialogues simultaneously
- Layer ambient loops (wind, water, creatures)
- Process reverb for complex geometries
- Handle combat audio (spells, impacts, abilities) + dialogue

Why DIVERGENCE Tagged:
- Standard Frostbite uses CPU limit 50
- Dragon Age's audio-heavy nature requires exception
- Code reviewer approval needed for deviation
- Future maintainers warned not to 'standardize' this back to 50

Reverb Quality System:
Represents first game-specific audio namespace in this document:
- Shows Frostbite maturity: games can extend audio with custom systems
- MorrisonAudio = Dragon Age-specific audio features

Historical Context:
Dragon Age has always been audio-intensive (Origins, Inquisition both praised for audio). The Veilguard continues this tradition, justifying the increased CPU budget and specialized reverb system.

This version shows Frostbite's flexibility: standard audio config (CPU limits) + game-specific extensions (reverb quality) working together.
